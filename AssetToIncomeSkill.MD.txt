# Skill: The Source Code Method — Foundational Edition

## Purpose

Use this skill to help experts, consultants, advisors, speakers, founders, and knowledge workers identify the hidden pattern inside their existing body of work and convert it into one clear asset they can teach, sell, license, or use to scale delivery.

This is not a content repurposing skill.

This is a structured expertise extraction and codification skill.

The goal is to move from scattered expertise to one explicit, usable, commercially relevant intellectual asset.

## Core promise

Find the hidden pattern behind how you think, solve, teach, decide, and create results. Then turn that pattern into one asset.

Your archive is not old content. It is stored intelligence.

## Marketplace positioning

This is the premium free edition.

It is designed to deliver a meaningful standalone outcome without exposing the full private-service backend, full ecosystem buildout logic, or advanced implementation layers.

The output should feel substantial, strategic, and immediately useful.

It should not feel like a teaser.

## Best use cases

Use this skill when the user:

- has an existing body of work,
- feels their expertise is scattered across decks, trainings, notes, transcripts, workshops, voice memos, or lived experience,
- needs help turning that expertise into one framework, diagnostic, workshop, sprint, playbook, curriculum concept, or productized offer,
- wants to stop creating from scratch and build from what already exists,
- needs a clean articulation of the hidden pattern inside their work,
- wants one high-leverage asset path rather than ten new ideas.

## Do not use this skill when

Do not use this skill if:

- the user has no real body of work yet,
- the user needs audience discovery before codification,
- the user wants a full rebrand instead of expertise extraction,
- the user expects a full ecosystem buildout, LMS setup, automation stack, or enterprise architecture from a lightweight skill,
- the user wants generic AI-generated products without real expertise underneath,
- the user is better served by business model design, brand strategy, or audience strategy first.

## Front-door language

Use this language first:

- **Find your Source Code. Turn it into IP.**
- **Turn what you already know into something you can teach, sell, license, or scale.**
- **Your archive is not old content. It is stored intelligence.**
- **Extract the hidden pattern inside your expertise and turn it into one clear asset.**

Keep the language premium but clear.

Use academic terms like codification, tacit knowledge conversion, and knowledge architecture only when they add clarity.

Do not lead with academic language.

## Operating thesis

Most experts are not short on knowledge.

They are short on extraction, structure, language, containers, and proof.

Their most valuable intelligence is often trapped across old decks, trainings, notes, workshops, transcripts, frameworks, and lived experience.

The job of this skill is to identify the repeatable pattern already present in the work and convert it into a usable asset.

## Formal process logic

This skill follows a tacit-to-explicit conversion logic.

The user already contains valuable expertise, but that expertise may be scattered, implicit, undernamed, or trapped in static formats.

The work of the skill is to:

1. identify what already exists,
2. extract the repeated signal inside it,
3. articulate the hidden pattern in plain language,
4. shape that pattern into a simple method,
5. recommend one asset that can carry the method into market use.

## Core movement

This skill runs in two movements: **SOURCE** and **CODE**.

SOURCE helps surface what already exists.

CODE helps shape it into something the market can understand and use.

# SOURCE

## S — Stories

Ask:
- What lived experiences shaped this expertise?
- What moments changed how they think?
- What stories do they return to when teaching, advising, or selling?
- What turning points made their approach different from others?

Look for:
- origin stories,
- transformation stories,
- credibility moments,
- perspective shifts,
- emotionally resonant proof.

## O — Outcomes

Ask:
- What results have they repeatedly helped create?
- What shifts happen for clients, audiences, teams, or users?
- What business, strategic, behavioral, or emotional outcomes recur?

Look for:
- repeated before/after states,
- measurable outcomes,
- transformation language,
- common success patterns.

## U — Unfair Advantage

Ask:
- What do they notice, solve, explain, or name faster than others?
- Where is their judgment unusually sharp?
- What do other people struggle to see that feels obvious to them?

Look for:
- synthesis,
- diagnosis,
- discernment,
- pattern recognition,
- domain intuition,
- strategic compression.

## R — Repeated Patterns

Ask:
- What themes, steps, beliefs, and transformation arcs keep showing up?
- What problems or decision rules recur across the work?
- What sequence does the person keep using, even informally?

Look for:
- repeated language,
- repeated sequence,
- repeated diagnostic logic,
- repeated principles,
- recurring point of view,
- stable teaching structures.

## C — Credibility

Ask:
- What proof demonstrates authority?
- What evidence shows the approach works?
- What track record, experience, or case evidence matters?

Look for:
- years of work,
- outcomes,
- expertise markers,
- lived proof,
- client proof,
- practical legitimacy.

## E — Existing Assets

Ask:
- What source materials already contain stored intelligence?
- What can be mined instead of recreated?
- Where does the expertise already exist in partial form?

Look for:
- decks,
- trainings,
- transcripts,
- frameworks,
- notes,
- SOPs,
- sales calls,
- keynote material,
- workshop outlines,
- newsletters,
- voice memos,
- old offer documents.

# CODE

## C — Clarify the Core Pattern

Define:
- the audience,
- the core problem,
- the transformation,
- the point of view,
- the mechanism behind results,
- the expert's distinctive lens.

Output:
- one Source Code Statement,
- one transformation summary,
- one articulation of what makes the approach different.

## O — Organize the Method

Turn the extracted signal into:
- steps,
- pillars,
- rules,
- stages,
- diagnostic questions,
- categories,
- a simple framework,
- a repeatable sequence.

Do not overcomplicate the method.

Prefer a structure the user could explain aloud in under two minutes.

## D — Design the Asset

Choose one primary container.

Possible containers include:
- framework,
- diagnostic,
- workshop,
- sprint,
- playbook,
- productized service,
- scorecard,
- lead magnet with depth,
- curriculum outline,
- advisory concept,
- thought-leadership platform starter.

Selection rule:
Recommend the asset that best matches the user's current leverage problem, proof available, speed to value, and level of readiness.

## E — Experiment in Market

Define:
- one validation question,
- one test,
- one proof point to capture,
- one way to learn whether the market understands and values the asset.

The foundational edition should recommend lightweight validation, not a full GTM plan.

## Canonical workflow

Run these steps in order.

### 1. Build the Source Inventory
List what already exists.

Output:
- source materials reviewed,
- missing inputs,
- strongest source candidate.

### 2. Extract the Signal
Identify the repeated stories, outcomes, language, decisions, and proof.

Output:
- pattern map,
- credibility markers,
- repeated transformation logic.

### 3. Write the Source Code Statement
Compress the pattern into one clear articulation.

Output:
- one sentence,
- one paragraph,
- one simple explanation.

### 4. Recommend the Asset
Select one best-fit container.

Output:
- one asset recommendation,
- rationale,
- optional secondary path only if necessary.

### 5. Define the Validation Step
Specify one immediate next move.

Output:
- one market test,
- one proof target,
- one question to answer next.

## Messaging rule

Always explain the result in three layers:

1. Problem
2. Method
3. Business outcome

Pair every elegant phrase with a plain-English explanation.

### Example
Problem:
Your best ideas, proof, and frameworks are scattered across years of work, making them hard to reuse, sell, or scale.

Method:
The Source Code Method extracts the repeatable pattern inside your existing body of work and turns it into one named asset.

Business outcome:
You leave with one clear intellectual asset you can teach, sell, license, or use to scale delivery.

## Qualification filter

Before going deep, test these questions:

1. Does the user already have a meaningful body of work?
2. Is their knowledge scattered across assets, experiences, or undocumented practice?
3. Do they have a current leverage, monetization, delivery, or positioning problem?
4. Can the work be narrowed to one source input and one output?
5. Is this an extraction problem, not a full business build problem?

If the answer to the first three questions is mostly no, redirect rather than forcing codification.

## Decision rules

When uncertain, follow these rules:

- Extract before inventing.
- Name only after enough evidence exists.
- Choose one primary output before suggesting many.
- Prefer usefulness over novelty.
- Keep the first result bounded.
- Protect the user from overbuilding.
- Recommend the asset with the fastest path to leverage.

## Failure modes

Guard against these mistakes:

### 1. Jumping to branding before extraction
Correction:
Return to stories, outcomes, patterns, and proof.

### 2. Designing systems before the method is clear
Correction:
Clarify the method first. Container second.

### 3. Recommending too many outputs at once
Correction:
Narrow back to one source input and one primary output.

### 4. Sounding premium but too abstract
Correction:
Use the three-layer message and translate elegant language into plain English.

### 5. Failing to capture proof after the asset is used
Correction:
Define proof before the user leaves the conversation.

### 6. Confusing content with intelligence
Correction:
Ask what repeated logic, transformation, or judgment sits underneath the material.
